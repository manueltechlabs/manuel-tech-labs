Today’s focus: Customer Segmentation in Practice — applying K-Means, DBSCAN, and Agglomerative Clustering to group customers by order frequency and average order value (AOV).  We’ll walk through model selection, evaluation, and business impact—using real code and insights from TheLook dataset.

## 🧠 Why Segment Customers? The Business Case
- Most marketing efforts treat all customers the same.
- We apply clustering to uncover hidden behavioral segments.
- Not all customers contribute equally—some spend big but rarely; others buy often but spend little.
- Personalized campaigns increase retention, AOV, and lifetime value. 

## 🔍 Step 1: Define Behavioral Metrics

We start by calculating two key indicators:

```sql
-- Order Frequency & AOV per User
WITH order_value AS (
  SELECT 
    o.user_id,
    o.order_id,
    SUM(oi.sale_price) AS total_order_value
  FROM `bigquery-public-data.thelook_ecommerce.orders` o
  JOIN `bigquery-public-data.thelook_ecommerce.order_items` oi
    ON o.order_id = oi.order_id
  WHERE o.delivered_at IS NOT NULL
  GROUP BY 1, 2
)
SELECT
  ov.user_id,
  COUNT(*) AS order_frequency,
  AVG(ov.total_order_value) AS avg_order_value
FROM order_value ov
GROUP BY 1
```

This gives us a clean input for clustering: (user_id, order_frequency, avg_order_value).

## 📈 Step 2: Choose the Right Algorithm

| Algorithm | Best For | Limitations |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **K-Means** | Convex, balanced clusters (e.g., RFM segments) | Assumes spherical clusters; sensitive to outliers |
| **DBSCAN** | Finding noise & irregular shapes | Struggles with varying densities |
| **Agglomerative** | Hierarchical relationships | Computationally heavy |

  💡 Insight: A 2023 UK retail study found Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) outperformed others with a Silhouette Score of 0.80, but K-Means remains ideal for interpretable business segments. 

## 🧪 Step 3: Find Optimal Clusters
There are two most popular methods Silhouette Score and the Elbow method, here we use the Elbow method:

### Elbow Method

```
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
inertias = []
for k in range(1, 11):
    kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42)
    kmeans.fit(df[['order_frequency', 'avg_order_value']])
    inertias.append(kmeans.inertia_)
```

Plotting this reveals an “elbow” at k=2 or 3, indicating optimal cluster count.

Big Spenders	Buy rarely, spend big	Retention emails, early access, VIP perks

## Cluster validation

Clusters were validated through visual inspection and alignment with real-world customer segmentation logic, such as purchase frequency and average order value. DBSCAN identified only one meaningful cluster, suggesting limited density separation in the data. For synthetic datasets like this, formal validation is constrained — but in real-world applications, cluster validity should be rigorously assessed using metrics such as:

- Silhouette Score: higher values indicate better-defined, separated clusters.
- Calinski-Harabasz Index: evaluates cluster dispersion; higher scores are better.
- Davies-Bouldin Index: measures cluster similarity; lower values indicate better partitioning.
- Visual inspection & domain knowledge: ensures clusters are interpretable and actionable.
